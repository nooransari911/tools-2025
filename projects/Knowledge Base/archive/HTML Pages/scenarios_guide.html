The Analyst’s Field Guide: Connecting Problems to Metrics

This guide is structured around the five most common categories of business problems you will receive in an interview. For each category, we will detail the core concepts you should use and provide a model answer using the Why, What, Then framework.

1. The “Something Dropped” Scenario

This is the most common type of diagnostic question. A key metric has unexpectedly fallen, and you are asked to be the detective. Examples include a drop in sales, daily active users, or conversion rates. The interviewer is testing your ability to perform a structured root cause analysis and not jump to conclusions.

Your Core Toolkit

The primary technique here is Segmentation. You are trying to isolate the drop from a general problem (“sales are down”) to a specific one (“sales are down for new users in Germany on Android devices”). Your goal is to find the “jagged edge” in the data.

  • Key Concepts:
    • The Funnel: To see where in the user journey the drop is happening.
    • User Segments: Slicing by WHO data (e.g., New vs. Returning users, Geography, Device Type, App Version).
    • Time-Series Analysis: To confirm if the drop is a real anomaly or just normal fluctuation.

Model Application: “Daily Active Users (DAU) dropped by 15%.”

The WHY: The Hypothesis
A sudden 15% drop in a core metric like DAU is rarely caused by a slow change in user behavior. My immediate hypothesis is that this is a technical problem, not a strategic one. The drop is likely not affecting all users equally but is instead concentrated in a specific segment. My job is to find that segment.

The WHAT: The Action
First, I would validate the data to ensure our tracking isn’t broken. Then, I would begin a structured segmentation. I would query our user activity data, grouping the DAU count by a prioritized list of dimensions, starting with the most likely technical culprits:

  1. Platform: Operating System (iOS vs. Android), Browser Type.
  2. Version: Application Version.
  3. Geography: Country.

This structured GROUP BY approach will allow me to quickly determine if the drop is isolated, for instance, to users who updated to our latest iOS version.

The THEN: The Next Step
If the data confirms the drop is isolated to a specific segment (e.g., iOS v3.4.1), I would immediately quantify the business impact—the absolute number of users affected. I would then package this specific, actionable finding into a high-priority ticket, tag the lead iOS engineer, and provide them with a clear chart showing the drop-off time correlated with the app’s release time. This turns a vague alarm into a precise target for the engineering team.

2. The “Is This Working?” Scenario

This type of question is about measuring the success of a specific action the company has taken. Examples include evaluating a new feature launch, a marketing campaign, or a website redesign. The interviewer is testing your ability to define “success” and connect product/marketing actions to business outcomes.

Your Core Toolkit

The primary technique here is Comparative Analysis. You need to compare the group of users who experienced the change (the “treatment group”) against those who didn’t (the “control group”). The goal is to measure the lift or change in behavior.

  • Key Concepts:
    • A/B Testing: The gold standard for measuring causality.
    • Feature Adoption Rate: To see if people are even using the new thing.
    • Engagement Metrics (Stickiness, Session Duration): To see if user behavior is improving.
    • Goal-Specific Metrics (CVR, AOV, Retention): To see if the feature is impacting the bottom line.

Model Application: “Was our new ‘Wishlist’ feature a success?”

The WHY: The Hypothesis
The success of a product feature isn’t just about usage; it’s about its impact on the business. My hypothesis is that a successful feature should not only be adopted by users but should also lead to positive changes in their core behaviors, such as keeping them around longer (Retention) and encouraging them to buy more (Monetization).

The WHAT: The Action
I would measure success across three pillars. First, for Adoption, I would calculate the feature’s adoption rate among our monthly active users. Second, for Engagement, I would conduct a comparative analysis. I’d create two cohorts: users who have engaged with the Wishlist feature and those who haven’t. I would then compare the average Session Duration and Stickiness (DAU/MAU) between these two groups. Third, for Business Impact, I would compare the LTV and Purchase Frequency of the Wishlist users versus the non-users.

The THEN: The Next Step
If the data shows that Wishlist users have a 10% higher retention rate and a 15% higher LTV, I can confidently report to the product team that the feature is a success and is creating more valuable customers. I would recommend they invest in making the feature more prominent. If, however, adoption is low, my recommendation would be to investigate why—is the feature hard to find, or is it not providing real value?

3. The “Which Should We Choose?” Scenario

This is a strategic question about prioritization and forecasting. You are given multiple options and are asked to use data to help the business decide where to invest its limited resources. The interviewer is testing your ability to use data to inform future decisions, not just analyze the past.

Your Core Toolkit

The primary technique is Opportunity Sizing. You are making a data-informed estimate of the potential impact of each option. Your goal is to provide a rational basis for comparison.

  • Key Concepts:
    • Funnel Analysis: To identify the biggest current point of friction, which represents the biggest opportunity.
    • Opportunity Sizing: Estimating the potential Reach * Impact of a change.
    • User Feedback Data (Support Tickets, Surveys): To gauge qualitative demand for a feature.

Model Application: “Should we improve our search algorithm or build a new social sharing feature?”

The WHY: The Hypothesis
This is a classic prioritization problem. We can’t do everything, so we need to place our bet on the initiative that will likely have the biggest positive impact on our core business goals. My approach would be to estimate the potential opportunity for each option. We should focus our efforts on the path that can impact the most users and solve the biggest existing problem.

The WHAT: The Action
To size the opportunity for improving search, I would first analyze the current search funnel. How many of our monthly active users use search? Of those, what is the ‘null result’ rate or the conversion rate for users who search? This will give me the total number of users who could be impacted (the Reach) and the potential upside in conversion (the Impact). For the social sharing feature, I would look at our marketing data. How much of our traffic currently comes from users sharing links on social media? This provides a baseline. I’d also check our support data to see if this is a highly requested feature.

The THEN: The Next Step
After sizing both opportunities, I would present them side-by-side to the product team. For example: “Improving search could impact the 30% of our users who search every month, and improving conversion by just 5% for this group could lead to an estimated $50k in new revenue. The social sharing feature, based on current referral traffic, might impact a smaller user base initially but could have a long-term viral growth effect.” This data-driven comparison allows the team to make a strategic trade-off based on potential impact versus risk.

This guide provides a structure, not a script. Recognize the pattern of the question, select the right tools from your arsenal, and use the Why, What, Then framework to tell a clear, logical story. This will demonstrate a level of structured thinking far beyond that of a typical fresher.
