Think Fast, Talk Smart: Communication Techniques

The “Think Fast, Talk Smart: Communication Techniques” workshop outlines strategies for effective spontaneous speaking, focusing on anxiety management, establishing ground rules, and implementing a four-step process for spontaneous communication.

Managing Anxiety

The workshop emphasizes that 85% of people report being nervous when speaking in public. This fear is ubiquitous, ranking among the top five fears for Americans. The goal is not to overcome anxiety, but to manage it, as anxiety can provide energy, focus, and signal the importance of the situation. A key responsibility of a communicator, whether speaking spontaneously or planned, is to make the audience comfortable so they can receive the message. When speakers are nervous, the audience often feels uncomfortable, awkward, or disengages, which does not help the speaker.

The workshop offers three techniques based on academic research to manage anxiety. The first is to greet your anxiety. When anxiety symptoms appear, such as a gurgling stomach, shaking legs, or perspiration, you should acknowledge them by saying, “Hey, this is me feeling nervous. I’m about to do something of consequence.” Simply greeting and acknowledging anxiety as normal can stem the tide of spiraling out of control.

A second technique is to reframe the situation as a conversation. Many speakers feel they are “performing,” which leads to a focus on getting it “right.” Instead, you should view presenting as a conversation. This can be achieved by starting with questions, which are dialogic and involve the audience, making the interaction feel more conversational. Speakers can use questions to outline presentations instead of bullet points. It is also important to use conversational language. Nervous speakers often use distancing language like, “one must consider,” or “today we’re going to cover step one.” Instead, use inclusive pronouns and conversational phrases such as, “this is important to you,” “we all need to be concerned with,” and “first what we need to do is this.”

The third technique is to become present-oriented. Worrying about future consequences like grades, funding, or laughs increases nervousness. Bringing yourself into the present moment can reduce this concern. There are several ways to do this. Physical activity, like a walk around the building or even doing push-ups, can force present orientation. Athletes often listen to music to get in the moment. You can also use cognitive tasks, such as counting backwards from a difficult number like 100 by 17s. Finally, saying a tongue twister like, “I slit a sheet, a sheet I slit, and on that slitted sheet I sit,” forces focus on the present moment and warms up the voice.

Ground Rules for the Workshop

The workshop is designed to be highly interactive and participative, with opportunities for attendees to talk to others and practice. It focuses on four critical steps for effective spontaneous speaking. Each step includes an activity, none of which are described as painful. The workshop also integrates phrases and sayings from the world of improvisation, as it is co-taught with an improv expert, to help remember techniques.

Speaking Spontaneously: A Four-Step Process

The workshop provides four steps to help individuals speak more comfortably in spontaneous situations.

Get Out of Your Own Way (“Dare to Be Dull”)

The first barrier to spontaneous speaking is often ourselves, due to a desire for perfection or to give the “right” answer. Type-A personalities often stockpile answers and over-plan, which hinders spontaneity. The workshop includes an activity called “Shout the Wrong Name” where participants point at objects and loudly call them anything but what they are, without pre-planning or creating patterns. This game trains the brain to get out of its own way and work against the muscle memory of problem-solving quickly, which can lead to overthinking. This is summarized by the maxim: “Dare to Be Dull“. Instead of striving for greatness, allow yourself to be dull. By daring to be dull and releasing the pressure of perfection, you can actually achieve greatness. This approach encourages responding genuinely and authentically rather than reacting with planned thoughts.

Reframe the Situation as an Opportunity (“Yes, And…”)

People often view speaking opportunities, especially Q&A sessions, as adversarial challenges or threats. Changing this perspective to an opportunity—to clarify, understand, explain, or expand—can lead to more freedom and different interactions. An activity to practice this is “Imaginary Gifts.” In it, partners exchange imaginary gifts, the receiver names whatever they “find” inside, and the giver must spontaneously explain why they gave that specific item. This game helps speakers see interactions as opportunities, have fun, and co-create. The surprise of the named gift allows for spontaneous and creative responses from the giver. The maxim for this step is “Yes, And.” This improvisation saying encourages an open, accepting approach to situations, fostering tremendous opportunities rather than being defensive with “no, but.”

Slow Down and Listen (“Don’t Just Do Something, Stand There”)

To respond appropriately, it is crucial to slow down and truly listen to understand the demands and needs of the audience. Speakers often jump ahead, listening only enough to formulate their own response. Fundamentally, a communicator’s job is to be in service of the audience. To practice this, an activity called “Spell Everything You Say” has partners hold a brief conversation where they spell out every word, such as “G-O-T A-T G-A-M-E.” This exercise forces focus and listening in the moment, preventing participants from thinking ahead and enabling a more targeted response. The associated maxim is “Don’t Just Do Something, Stand There,” which encourages listening first before responding.

Tell a Story (Use Structures)

Successful spontaneous speaking requires a structure to organize the message. Structure increases processing fluency, meaning structured information is processed about 40% more effectively and efficiently. It helps the speaker remember their points and keeps the audience on track. The workshop presents two useful structures. The first is “Problem, Solution, Benefit,” where you start with the issue, then explain the solution, and conclude with the benefits. The second is “What? So What? Now What?,” where you explain what it is, then why it’s important, and finally, what the next steps are. An activity to practice this is “Sell a Slinky,” where participants use one of the two structures to sell an imaginary Slinky to a partner. This practice helps speakers become comfortable with structures, which reduces the cognitive load of figuring out both what to say and how to say it. The maxim here is “Structure Sets You Free,” because by providing a framework, structure frees the speaker to focus on the content.

By applying these techniques—managing anxiety and practicing the four steps—individuals can become more compelling, confident, and connected speakers.
